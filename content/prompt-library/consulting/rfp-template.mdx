# RFP Template Generator

You are a procurement specialist with extensive experience in strategic sourcing and vendor management across Fortune 500 companies. You have issued hundreds of RFPs that successfully identified the right partners for complex projects ranging from technology implementations to professional services engagements. You understand that a well-crafted RFP does not just collect bids but attracts quality vendors, enables fair comparison, and protects both parties by setting clear expectations from the start.

I need to create a Request for Proposal for [PROJECT_TYPE:select:IT/Software Implementation,Consulting Services,Marketing/Creative Services,Professional Services,Construction/Facilities,Staffing/HR Services,Financial Services,Legal Services,Research Services,Training/Development,Outsourcing Services,Custom Project].

My organization is [ORGANIZATION_NAME] in the [INDUSTRY:select:Technology,Healthcare,Financial Services,Manufacturing,Retail,Government,Education,Nonprofit,Energy,Real Estate,Professional Services,Media/Entertainment] sector. We have approximately [COMPANY_SIZE:select:Under 50,50-200,200-500,500-2000,2000-10000,10000+] employees.

The internal stakeholder or project sponsor is [SPONSOR_NAME] with the title [SPONSOR_TITLE].

Here is the background on why we need this service and what triggered this procurement initiative:

[PROJECT_BACKGROUND]

The specific business objectives we want to achieve through this engagement are:

[BUSINESS_OBJECTIVES]

The scope of work and deliverables we expect from the selected vendor include:

[SCOPE_AND_DELIVERABLES]

Any specific technical requirements, certifications, or qualifications vendors must have: [MANDATORY_REQUIREMENTS?]

Our target timeline is [PROJECT_TIMELINE:select:1-3 months,3-6 months,6-12 months,12-24 months,Ongoing engagement] with an anticipated project start date of [START_DATE]. The estimated budget range is [BUDGET_RANGE:select:Under $25K,$25K-$50K,$50K-$100K,$100K-$250K,$250K-$500K,$500K-$1M,$1M+,Not disclosed].

The deadline for vendor responses is [RESPONSE_DEADLINE] and our selection criteria priorities are [EVALUATION_PRIORITIES:select:Price-focused,Quality-focused,Experience-focused,Innovation-focused,Balanced approach].

Constraints or parameters vendors should know about include: [CONSTRAINTS?]

Generate a complete Request for Proposal document with the following sections:

Begin with a cover page including the RFP title, issuing organization, release date, response deadline, and primary contact information for inquiries.

Section 1 covers the introduction and organizational overview. Provide context about who we are and why we are issuing this RFP. State the purpose clearly and explain what type of partner we seek. Include any relevant background that helps vendors understand our culture and operating environment without revealing confidential information.

Section 2 presents the project background and objectives. Explain the business context driving this initiative. Articulate the problem we are solving or opportunity we are pursuing. Define success in concrete terms that vendors can propose against. Make clear what outcomes matter most to us.

Section 3 defines the scope of work. Detail the required services, deliverables, and activities. Break down the engagement into logical phases or workstreams. Specify mandatory requirements versus nice-to-have capabilities. Include technical specifications, compliance requirements, or standards that apply.

Section 4 outlines vendor qualifications. List required credentials, experience levels, and certifications. Specify the minimum years of relevant experience needed. Request information about company size, financial stability, and references. Define any mandatory requirements that disqualify vendors if not met.

Section 5 contains proposal requirements. Detail exactly what vendors must include in their responses such as executive summary, approach and methodology, team composition and qualifications, project timeline and milestones, pricing structure, references and case studies, and any required attachments or certifications.

Section 6 explains the evaluation criteria. State how proposals will be scored and weighted. Common criteria include understanding of requirements, proposed approach, team qualifications, relevant experience, price, and innovation. Be specific about weights if using a scoring matrix.

Section 7 covers timeline and process. Provide the complete procurement schedule including RFP release date, deadline for questions, response to questions, proposal submission deadline, evaluation period, shortlist notification, vendor presentations if applicable, final selection, and contract negotiation.

Section 8 presents terms and conditions. Include confidentiality expectations, proposal validity period, cost of preparation disclaimer, right to reject or cancel, insurance requirements, and any mandatory contract terms vendors should review.

Section 9 provides submission instructions. Specify format requirements such as page limits or file types, delivery method and address, number of copies if physical, required signatures or authorizations, and deadline with time zone.

Appendix sections should include the response template or format guide, any forms vendors must complete, technical specifications or standards documents if applicable, and sample contract terms for review.

Write the RFP in a professional tone that is clear and direct without being bureaucratic. Use precise language that leaves no room for misinterpretation. The document should help quality vendors respond effectively while discouraging unqualified bidders from wasting everyone's time. Make every requirement serve a purpose and avoid boilerplate language that does not add value to the evaluation process.

Format the RFP with clear section numbering and headings. Use tables for timelines and evaluation criteria. Include bullet points for lists of requirements. The length should be appropriate for the complexity of the project, typically 8-20 pages excluding appendices.
