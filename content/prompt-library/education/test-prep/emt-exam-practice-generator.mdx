# EMT Exam (NREMT) Practice Generator

The NREMT EMT cognitive exam runs as a computer adaptive test, which means the number of questions and the exact difficulty shift in real time based on how a candidate answers, and no two test takers see the same exam. That uncertainty is often harder to prepare for than the content itself. This prompt writes original EMT-style practice questions, never copied from the National Registry's official exam.

Set [DOMAIN:select:Scene Size-Up,Primary Assessment,Secondary Assessment,Patient Treatment and Transport,Operations,Mixed across all domains] to choose the content area and [QUESTION_COUNT:number:10-40] for how many questions to generate.

Write each question against one of the five current NREMT content domains. Scene Size-Up covers hazard identification, standard precautions, and initial scene safety decisions. Primary Assessment covers the initial patient evaluation, airway, breathing, circulation, and identifying life threats. Secondary Assessment covers detailed history taking and focused physical exams. Patient Treatment and Transport covers interventions, medication administration within EMT scope, and transport decisions. Operations covers ambulance operations, incident command, and EMS system roles. If [DOMAIN] is mixed, distribute [QUESTION_COUNT] roughly in proportion to how heavily each domain is weighted on the real exam, with Primary Assessment and Patient Treatment and Transport getting the largest share.

Mix traditional multiple choice with newer formats the exam has adopted, multiple-select items with more than one correct answer, and build-a-list items asking for the correct sequence of actions in a scenario. Provide a full answer key with a rationale explaining the clinical reasoning behind each correct answer, not just which option is right.

Close by noting that because the real exam is adaptive and can stop anywhere between 70 and 120 questions once it reaches statistical confidence in a pass or fail result, question count on any single practice run should never be treated as a target to hit, only as a volume of practice.
