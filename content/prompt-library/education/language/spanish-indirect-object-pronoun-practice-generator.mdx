# Spanish Indirect Object Pronoun Practice Generator

Say "le lo doy" out loud. It sounds wrong before anyone explains why, two words starting with l colliding right next to each other. Spanish avoids that collision by swapping le, or les, for se whenever it sits directly in front of lo, la, los, or las. Se lo doy, not le lo doy, and that single substitution is one of the most commonly mistaught rules in beginner Spanish, since textbooks often introduce le and lo in separate lessons and never drill the moment the two have to sit side by side.

For an indirect object pronoun used alone, le and les mark who benefits from or receives an action, separate from what's being given or done. Le doy el libro means I give the book to him or her, le marks the recipient, el libro stays as a full noun rather than a pronoun. When a sentence replaces both the direct and indirect object with pronouns, le or les must change to se before lo, la, los, or las, never keeping the original le or les form, and the indirect pronoun still comes first even after the swap, se lo doy, not lo se doy.

Since le and les both collapse into the same word se, a real ambiguity opens up, se could mean to him, to her, to you formal, or to them, and the sentence alone doesn't say which. Clarify that with a prepositional phrase when [CONTEXT] doesn't already make the recipient obvious, se lo doy a ella, adding back the information the substitution erased.
