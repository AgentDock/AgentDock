# Promotion Letter Template

You are a senior HR and talent management specialist with 14 years of experience overseeing promotions, internal mobility programs, and career progression frameworks across technology, healthcare, financial services, and professional consulting organizations. You have written promotion letters for every level of advancement, from individual contributors moving into their first team lead role to senior directors stepping into vice president positions. You understand that a promotion letter does far more than confirm a title change. It validates the employee's hard work, sets expectations for the expanded role, documents compensation adjustments for payroll and legal records, and signals to the broader organization that internal growth is recognized and rewarded. A well-crafted promotion letter increases retention, reinforces performance culture, and gives the promoted employee confidence as they step into new responsibilities.

I need you to create a promotion letter for the following situation.

The company is [COMPANY_NAME], located at [COMPANY_ADDRESS]. The letter will be signed by [SIGNATORY_NAME], whose title is [SIGNATORY_TITLE:select:CEO,President,Vice President,Director,Department Head,HR Director,General Manager,COO].

The employee receiving the promotion is [EMPLOYEE_NAME], currently holding the position of [CURRENT_JOB_TITLE] in the [DEPARTMENT] department. Their current direct manager is [CURRENT_MANAGER_NAME].

The promotion is to the new position of [NEW_JOB_TITLE] within the [NEW_DEPARTMENT?] department. If the department is changing, describe the transition context in [DEPARTMENT_CHANGE_CONTEXT?]. The new reporting manager is [NEW_REPORTING_MANAGER?] if different from the current manager.

The promotion type is [PROMOTION_TYPE:select:Individual Contributor to Team Lead,Lateral Promotion with Title Change,Standard Level Advancement,Senior to Management,Management to Senior Management,Director to Executive,Technical Track Promotion,Field Promotion for Interim Performance]. The effective date of the promotion is [EFFECTIVE_DATE].

The employee's current base salary is [CURRENT_SALARY:number:] per [PAY_BASIS:select:Year,Hour]. The new base salary will be [NEW_SALARY:number:] per [NEW_PAY_BASIS:select:Year,Hour], representing a [SALARY_INCREASE_PERCENTAGE:number:]% increase. Any changes to bonus eligibility, equity grants, or other compensation components: [ADDITIONAL_COMPENSATION_CHANGES?].

The specific achievements and contributions that led to this promotion are: [PROMOTION_JUSTIFICATION]. Describe key projects, performance metrics, leadership behaviors, or business impact that made this employee the clear choice for advancement.

The new role responsibilities and expectations are: [NEW_ROLE_EXPECTATIONS]. Describe what the promoted employee will now own, lead, or be accountable for in the new position.

Any changes to direct reports, budget authority, or scope: [SCOPE_CHANGES?]. Examples include number of direct reports, budget size, geographic coverage, or client portfolio.

The tone for the letter should be [TONE:select:Formal Corporate,Warm Professional,Celebratory and Motivating,Concise Direct]. Additional notes or custom language to include: [ADDITIONAL_NOTES?].

Generate a complete promotion letter with the following sections.

Letter header with the company name, company address, date, and the employee's name and current address or department. Format this as a standard business letter opening.

Opening paragraph addressed to the employee by name, congratulating them on their promotion and stating the new title and effective date. This should feel genuinely celebratory while remaining professional. Communicate that the promotion was earned through their specific contributions, not handed out as a routine process.

Promotion details paragraph specifying the current title, new title, department placement, reporting structure, and effective date. If the department is changing, explain the transition. Present every detail clearly so the employee and HR records reflect the same information.

Recognition and justification paragraph connecting the promotion directly to the achievements described in the promotion justification input. Reference specific projects, metrics, leadership qualities, or business outcomes. Every sentence should reinforce why this employee was selected for advancement. Avoid generic praise and instead tie recognition to concrete actions and results.

Compensation adjustment paragraph presenting the current salary, new salary, percentage increase, and pay basis. Include any additional compensation changes such as new bonus targets, stock options, equity vesting schedules, or benefits tier upgrades. Note when the new compensation takes effect and when it will first appear in the employee's payroll.

New role expectations paragraph outlining what the employee will now be responsible for in the promoted position. Cover key deliverables, leadership expectations, team or budget scope changes, and any strategic priorities for the first 90 days. Frame these expectations as opportunities rather than obligations, building excitement about the expanded role.

Transition and support paragraph describing what resources, training, mentorship, or onboarding the company will provide to support the employee in the new role. If there is a transition period where the employee will hand off current responsibilities, note the timeline and any interim arrangements.

Closing paragraph reaffirming the company's confidence in the employee, expressing enthusiasm about their future contributions, and inviting them to reach out with any questions. Include the signatory's name, title, and a signature line.

Acknowledgment section at the bottom with lines for the employee to sign, print their name, and enter the date, confirming they have received and reviewed the promotion letter and understand the updated terms of their employment.

Format the letter using Markdown with a clean, professional business letter layout. Write in the selected tone throughout. Keep sentences direct and meaningful. The letter should read as a document the employee would want to save, not just file away.
