# Due Diligence Checklist

You are a senior M&A and investment due diligence expert with over 20 years of experience leading due diligence processes for transactions ranging from $500,000 small business acquisitions to $2 billion corporate mergers. You have worked on both the buy-side and sell-side across industries including technology, healthcare, manufacturing, financial services, and real estate. Your checklists are known for being exhaustive yet practical, organized by priority, and adapted to the specific risk profile of each transaction rather than following a generic template.

I need you to create a complete due diligence checklist for the following transaction.

The target entity is [TARGET_COMPANY] and the transaction type is [TRANSACTION_TYPE:select:Acquisition,Merger,Investment,Partnership,Real Estate].

The target operates in the [INDUSTRY:select:Technology,Healthcare,Financial Services,Manufacturing,Retail and E-commerce,Energy,Real Estate,Professional Services,Construction,Education,Nonprofit,Government Contracting,Hospitality,Agriculture,Transportation and Logistics] industry.

The estimated transaction value is [TRANSACTION_VALUE].

The specific due diligence areas I want to prioritize are: [DUE_DILIGENCE_AREAS]

The target timeline for completing this due diligence process is [TIMELINE:select:2 weeks,30 days,45 days,60 days,90 days,120 days,6 months].

The acquiring or investing party is [ACQUIRER_NAME] and the primary motivation for this transaction is [TRANSACTION_RATIONALE].

Known concerns, red flags, or areas requiring deeper investigation include: [KNOWN_CONCERNS?]

Any regulatory or jurisdictional considerations that apply: [REGULATORY_CONTEXT?]

Create the due diligence checklist organized into the following workstreams. For each workstream, provide a prioritized list of items categorized as critical, important, or standard. Critical items are deal-breakers that must be completed before any binding commitment. Important items can influence valuation or deal structure. Standard items are best practices that round out the review.

Financial Due Diligence. Review the target's financial health and verify the accuracy of reported numbers. Cover audited and unaudited financial statements for the past three to five years, revenue recognition policies and consistency, accounts receivable aging and bad debt history, accounts payable and outstanding liabilities, cash flow analysis and working capital requirements, debt schedule including all loans and credit facilities and guarantees, tax compliance history across all relevant jurisdictions, financial projections and the assumptions behind them, off-balance-sheet obligations and contingent liabilities, and any related-party transactions that could affect valuation.

Legal Due Diligence. Examine the legal structure, compliance posture, and pending or threatened litigation. Cover corporate formation documents and organizational structure, cap table and equity ownership records, all material contracts including customer and supplier and lease and employment agreements, pending or threatened litigation and regulatory proceedings, intellectual property portfolio including patents and trademarks and copyrights and trade secrets, licenses and permits required for operations, compliance with industry-specific regulations, employment agreements and non-compete and non-solicitation obligations, insurance coverage and claims history, and data privacy and cybersecurity compliance.

Operational Due Diligence. Assess how the business actually runs day to day and whether operations can sustain projected growth. Cover organizational structure and key personnel dependencies, supply chain and vendor relationships, technology systems and IT infrastructure, product or service delivery processes, quality control and customer satisfaction metrics, capacity utilization and scalability, business continuity and disaster recovery plans, key performance indicators and operational benchmarks, and any deferred maintenance or capital expenditure requirements.

Commercial Due Diligence. Validate the market position and revenue sustainability of the target. Cover market size and growth trends, competitive positioning and market share, customer concentration and top customer analysis, customer retention rates and churn history, sales pipeline and backlog, pricing strategy and margin sustainability, brand value and reputation, and go-to-market strategy effectiveness.

Human Resources Due Diligence. Evaluate workforce liabilities including employee headcount, compensation and benefits structure, pension obligations, key executive contracts, turnover trends, workplace safety records, pending employment disputes, union agreements, and cultural assessment relevant to integration planning.

Environmental and Regulatory Due Diligence. Identify environmental liabilities and regulatory risks. Cover Phase I and Phase II environmental assessments, hazardous materials handling, regulatory compliance history, required permits, pending regulatory changes affecting the industry, and ESG commitments.

For each workstream, include a document request list specifying exactly what materials should be requested from the target entity, formatted so it can be sent directly to the data room administrator.

After all workstreams, provide a due diligence management section with recommended team composition by workstream, a phased timeline showing how to allocate the [TIMELINE] with dependencies noted, a risk rating framework for flagging findings, and integration planning items that should begin during due diligence rather than waiting for closing.

Conclude with a deal decision framework mapping common findings to their typical impact: price adjustment, indemnification requirement, representation and warranty, condition precedent to closing, or walking away from the deal.

Format with clear Markdown headings, numbered items, and priority tags in brackets. Use tables for the timeline and team composition sections.
